That old dog-eared loop doesn't get the final word.

After a brain injury, the mind can get stuck. The same worry, over and over, going nowhere. It isn't weakness, and it isn't permanent. This page sets out what that loop is, why an injured brain does it, what has actually helped, and a free app built to walk you through it.

Understanding it

What rumination after ABI actually is

Not ordinary worry. Something stickier.

Everyone worries sometimes. This is different. This is the same thought, or the same handful of thoughts, playing on repeat — often for hours, often about something you can do nothing about right now, often starting up again the moment you notice it's stopped. You can be halfway through a completely different task and find it's crept back in, quietly, without you inviting it.

It doesn't solve anything. That's the strange, exhausting part. Ordinary thinking works towards an answer. This loop doesn't. It just goes round. And because it doesn't resolve, it doesn't stop on its own — it needs something from outside itself to break the circuit.

If you've had this since your injury, it is not a character flaw, and it is not a sign you're losing your grip. It's an extremely common part of what an injured brain does while it's healing. Knowing that alone won't switch it off. But it's worth sitting with for a moment, because most people carrying this loop are also quietly carrying shame about having it — and that shame is not yours to carry.

Why it happens

Why the injured brain does this

Two systems tend to take the hit after a brain injury, and this loop lives in the gap between them.

The first is attention — your brain's ability to notice, moment to moment, what it's actually doing. In a well-rested, uninjured brain, this runs quietly in the background, catching you when you've drifted and gently pulling you back. After an injury, that noticing system can become sluggish. The mind wanders into the loop and the alarm that would normally flag it — you're doing this again — doesn't fire the way it used to. You can be forty minutes into a loop before you realise you're in one.

The second is regulation — the system that's supposed to switch off a stress response once the danger has passed. A worry lands, your body reacts as though there's a real and immediate threat, and normally that reaction fades once your thinking brain confirms there's nothing to actually do right now. After an injury, that "stand down" signal can get weaker. The alarm stays live. The brain keeps returning to the worry because, as far as your nervous system is concerned, it never got the all-clear.

Neither of these is permanent. This matters enough to say plainly: the brain is not fixed. It keeps rewiring itself in response to what you actually, repeatedly ask it to do — for better or worse. That's not a slogan, it's how the organ works. The same mechanism that can wear a loop into a groove is the mechanism that can wear a new path instead. It takes repetition, not willpower, and it takes longer than anyone wants it to. But the direction of travel is real.

What helps

Brian's approach to breaking the loop

Nothing here is clever. It's a small number of specific, repeatable moves — used again and again, because repetition is what actually rewires anything.

1. Notice it

Catch that you're driving the bus, not the other way round

The first move isn't to fight the loop. It's simply to notice you're in one. A quiet check-in — what's my mind actually doing right now — done a few times a day, is enough to start catching the loop earlier and earlier each time. You're not trying to answer anything deep. You're just looking.

2. Name it

Call it neurological static, and remember thought is not fact

Give it a name that separates it from you. This isn't a true crisis playing out in real time — it's neurological static, a glitch in a system that's still healing. And the thought itself, however loud, is not automatically a fact. Noticing the difference between "I'm having this thought" and "this thought is true" takes some of the weight out of it.

3. Give it an appointment

The worry doesn't have to be dealt with right now

Instead of trying to force the thought away — which rarely works and often makes it louder — give it a specific later time. I'll think about this properly at 6pm. The mind, oddly, will often accept this deal. The worry isn't being denied. It's being scheduled.

And sometimes now genuinely is the right time — the deal isn't always "later," it's a real appointment, kept. That means actually sitting with it when it comes due: what's true, what was your mind making worse than it is, what (if anything) actually needs doing. Give it a short, honest window, then close it and move on. And if, when the moment comes, the worry's simply lost its grip and there's nothing left to say — that counts too. Letting it go quietly is keeping the deal just as much as working through it is.

If you don't know where to start, borrow a sentence: What's true is… The story my brain added is… One thing I can actually do about it is… If a friend brought me this worry, I'd tell them… Any one of those is usually enough to get the appointment moving, and every appointment ends one of two honest ways — with an action, or with "that's just static."

You can also choose one person once — a friend, a family member, a therapist — and send them the worry and how it resolved, straight through your phone's own messaging or email. Nothing else leaves your device. If there's no one you'd want to send it to, that's alright too — Headway's helpline is there for exactly this, free and confidential, 0808 800 2244, Monday to Friday, 9 to 5.

4. Get it out of your head

Write it down, and let the page hold it for a while

A loop that's only inside your head has nowhere to go but round again. Writing it down — even badly, even just a scrawl — moves it from the inside of your skull to somewhere outside it. It doesn't have to be solved on the page. It just has to be out of your head.

5. Move your body

A short walk does more than it looks like it does

Rhythmic movement — even ten minutes, even slow — is one of the few things that reliably interrupts a loop from the outside in. You don't need a route, a pace, or a destination. You need your feet moving and your eyes on something other than the inside of your own head.

6. Protect your sleep

The loop bites hardest on a flat battery

Everything above gets harder when you're running on empty. A loop that's manageable on a rested day can feel unbeatable on a poorly-slept one. Protecting a regular bedtime and getting outside in the morning light isn't a side project — it's part of the same work.

None of these six moves is complicated on its own. What makes them work is doing the small one, over and over, rather than waiting for the big insight that fixes everything at once. — the approach behind LoopBreak

The tool

LoopBreak — a calm companion for the loop

LoopBreak is a free, single-page web app built from the strategies above. It's peer-designed self-help — built by an ABI survivor, for people living with the same loop — not a medical device and not a replacement for therapy. It's deliberately plain: flat navigation, large buttons, no streaks, no scores, no memory tests. Nothing to fail at.

Open LoopBreak loopbreak.help
  1. 1

    Get it out of my head — Brain Dump

    Write the looping thought down, just as it is. Once you save it, it locks away in the Worry Box until its appointment comes due — you can't read it back and feed it in the meantime. When it unlocks, that's the appointment: a single screen showing what you locked away, space to write what's actually true, what your mind made worse than it was, and what (if anything) needs doing — with sentence-starters if you need a way in ("What's true is…", "The story my brain added is…", "One thing I can actually do about it is…"). A gentle ten-minute window, then "That's dealt with — move on," which flows straight into a small physical task. Kept appointments are saved under "Dealt with." And if there's nothing left to say when it unlocks — the loop's simply lost its grip — letting it go counts as keeping the deal too. You can also choose one person once, and send them the worry and how it resolved through your own phone's apps — nothing else leaves the device.

  2. 2

    Break the loop — Circuit Breaker

    A guided, one-step-at-a-time walkthrough of Brian's own four-step routine: name the glitch as neurological static, remind yourself thought is not fact, then give the worry an appointment — later, via the Worry Box, or right now with "Deal with it right now," for when now genuinely is the moment. Either way it ends the same: a pivot to a small physical task in the real world.

  3. 3

    Ground me

    Visual box breathing (in for 4, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4) and a 5-4-3-2-1 sensory reset, taken one simple screen at a time. No timers to beat. No wrong way to do it.

  4. 4

    Energy check

    A two-tap check-in: Low, Charging, or Full. If you tap Low, the app suggests a 15-minute rest — because the loop bites hardest on a flat battery, and rest is a legitimate answer, not a failure.

Not only an ABI thing

If this sounds familiar, but you didn't have a brain injury

LoopBreak was built for and with an ABI survivor, and his voice runs through it. But the loop it describes — the same thought, going nowhere, refusing to switch off — isn't exclusive to acquired brain injury. People recovering from stroke, living with PTSD, or carrying the weight of a chronic illness or a hard stretch of life often describe exactly the same pattern.

We're not going to claim expertise in those experiences, or rewrite this guide to speak for them. But if what you've just read felt familiar, the strategies above and the app below were built to be used by anyone who recognises the loop, not just by people with the same diagnosis as the person who designed it.

Before you go

What this is, and what it isn't

This page and the LoopBreak app are lived-experience self-help, written and built by an ABI survivor and a friend who supported him. They are not a medical device, not a clinical treatment, and not a substitute for therapy or a proper assessment. They're one thing that has genuinely helped — offered in case it helps you too.

If you're worried about your own recovery, or the loop feels like it's getting worse rather than better, that's worth raising with your GP or your rehabilitation team. You don't need to have a crisis to ask for help — noticing something isn't right is reason enough.

If things feel urgent right now — if you feel unsafe, or you're thinking about harming yourself — please reach out to a real person straight away. This page can't help you in a crisis. A phone call can.
